Meaning of Geography
    [three_force_vectors.png]
    • Most people think that geography is only a study of:
      • Place names
      • Lengths of rivers
      • Heights of mountains
      • Names of countries and capital cities
    • However, geography is much more than this
    • Geography does not have a single definition that is universally accepted
    • It is difficult to forward a definition acceptable to all geographers at all times and places because of:
      • The dynamic nature of the discipline
      • Changes in its scope and method of study

    Origin of the Term

    • Ancient Greeks defined geography for the first time
    • They combined two words:
      • Geo → means earth
      • Graphic → means writing
    • Therefore, their definition of geography was "description of the earth"
    • However, geography is more than just the description of the earth
    • This definition seems very general when compared to the scope of current geography

    Commonly Acceptable Definition

    "The study of the spatial distribution of both physical and human-made things and phenomena on the earth's surface and the two-way interactions and interdependences between natural and human environments."

    Working Definition

    Geography is the scientific study of the Earth that describes and analyses spatial and temporal variations of physical, biological and human phenomena, and their interrelationships and dynamism over the surface of the Earth.

Definition, Scope and Themes
The Scope, Approaches and Themes of Geography
  • Geography is the science that explains:
    • The arrangements of various natural and cultural features on the Earth's surface
  • It is a holistic and interdisciplinary field contributing to:
    • The understanding of the changing spatial structures from the past to the future
  • Scope → the range and variety of contents which are included in a subject or field of study
  • The scope of geography is very wide and dynamic
  • Geography has a greater scope than mere locations → it treats a wide range of phenomena on the planet earth

The Geo-Sphere

  • Generally, the geo-sphere is considered as geography's scope
  • Geo-sphere → the combined physical and human spheres that make up the Earth system
  • The geo-sphere itself is made up of five sub-spheres
Geospheres Description Geography's Related Area of Study
Lithosphere The solid part → the rock layers of the earth Geomorphology, soil geography
Troposphere The lower part of the atmosphere where weather changes occur Climatology
Hydrosphere The water surfaces of the earth including oceans, seas and lakes Oceanography
Biosphere The part of the earth that supports all sorts of life Biogeography
Anthroposphere The earth's cultural landscape Cultural geography, population geography
  • In its scope, geography ranges from:
    • Physical sciences → Astronomy, Climatology, Geology, Botany, Zoology
    • Human studies → Anthropology, Ethnology, Sociology, History

Two Continuums in Geography

  • Continuum → a continuous sequence or range between two extremes or opposites
  • Geography can be approached by considering two continuums:
    • A physical-human continuum
    • A topical-regional continuum
Example of Continuum: Think of a light switch that dims gradually rather than just ON/OFF. Similarly, geography moves gradually from physical topics (like rocks and weather) to human topics (like culture and economy), not as separate boxes.
Fig: Geography continuums and related fields
[geography_continuum.png]
  • Topical (systematic) continuum → the study of one particular topic of physical or human analysis as it varies from place to place
    • Examples → Economic Geography, Cultural Geography, Urban Geography
Example of Topical Approach: A geographer studies climate patterns (one topic) across different countries → how temperature and rainfall vary from Ethiopia to Kenya to Egypt.
  • Regional continuum → the study of a particular region, both its human and environmental attributes
    • Examples → East Africa, Africa, Southeast Asia
Example of Regional Approach: A geographer studies East Africa as a whole → looking at its mountains, rivers, climate, people, culture, economy, and history all together.

Five Fundamental and Unifying Themes in Geography
[three_force_vectors.png]
  • Geography has five basic themes:
    • Location
    • Place
    • Human-Environment Interaction
    • Movement
    • Region
  • Themes → main subjects being discussed or described in geography

Theme 1: Location

  • Location → a particular place or position
  • Two types of location:
    • Relative Location
    • Absolute Location
Relative Location → based on the location of some physical or human-created feature
Example: "My house is near the school" or "Ethiopia is south of Eritrea"
Absolute Location → based on a coordinate grid system that gives a precise location on the earth's surface
Example: Addis Ababa is located at 9°N latitude and 38°E longitude
The most common coordinate grid system used is the latitude-longitude coordinate grid system found on most maps and globes

Theme 2: Human-Environment Interaction

  • Humans have always been in ceaseless interaction with their natural environment
  • Human-environment interaction involves three distinct aspects:
    • Dependency
    • Adaptation
    • Modification
Dependency → the ways in which humans are dependent on nature for a living
Example: Farmers depend on rainfall for crop growth; fishermen depend on rivers and oceans for fish
Adaptation → how humans modify themselves, their lifestyles and their behavior to live in a new environment with new challenges
Example: People living in cold regions wear warm clothes and build insulated houses
Modification → allowed humans to "conquer" the world for their comfortable living
Example: Building dams to store water, cutting forests to create farmland, constructing roads and buildings

Theme 3: Place

  • Place → refers to the physical and human aspects of a location
  • This theme of geography is associated with:
    • Toponym → the name of a place
    • Site → the description of the features of the place
    • Situation → the environmental conditions of the place
  • Each place in the world has its unique characteristics expressed in terms of:
    • Landforms, hydrology, biogeography, pedology
    • Characteristics and size of its human population
    • Distinct human cultures
  • The concept of "place" aids geographers to compare and contrast two places on Earth

Physical Characteristics of a Place

  • Includes a description of:
    • Mountains, rivers, beaches, topography
    • Climate, animal and plant life
Example of Physical Characteristics: The Ethiopian Highlands have rugged mountains, deep valleys, cool temperatures, and unique wildlife

Human Characteristics of a Place

  • Includes:
    • Land use, architectural styles, forms of livelihood
    • Religious practices, political systems, common foods
    • Local folklore, means of transportation, methods of communication
Example of Human Characteristics: Addis Ababa has modern buildings, traditional markets, Ethiopian Orthodox churches, mosques, and a mix of Amharic and other languages

Theme 4: Movement

  • Movement → the translocation of human beings, their goods, and their ideas from one end of the planet to another
  • It includes:
    • The physical movement of people
    • Transport of goods and services
    • Flow of ideas that allows the unification of human civilization and promotes its growth and prosperity
Example of Movement: People migrating from rural to urban areas; goods shipped from China to Africa; ideas spread through the internet and social media

Theme 5: Region

  • Region → an area of the earth's surface with similar characteristics
  • Regions usually have more than one characteristic that unifies them
  • These may include physical, political, economic or cultural characteristics
  • Geographers categorize regions into three types

Type 1: Formal (Uniform) Regions

  • Characterized by a common human property or common physical property
  • Based on measurable data
Examples of Formal Regions:
Human property: People who share a language (Arabic-speaking region), religion (Christian region), nationality, or political identity
Physical property: Climate (tropical region), landform (mountain region), vegetation (forest region)

Type 2: Functional (Nodal) Regions

  • A region made up of different places that are linked and function as a unit
  • Characterized by a hub or central place
  • Defined by the linkages binding particular phenomena
Example of Functional Region: Addis Ababa is the center of Ethiopia linked with other regions through roads, trade, communication, and administration
Another Example: A newspaper circulation area → the main city (hub) distributes newspapers to surrounding towns (linked places)

Type 3: Perceptual (Vernacular) Regions

  • A region in which people perceive or see the characteristics of a region in the same way
  • Based on human feelings and attitudes
Example of Perceptual Region: "Piassa" is considered the middle of Addis Ababa by many residents, based on their perception and shared understanding
Another Example: "The South" in the United States → people have shared perceptions about culture, food, and lifestyle, even without precise boundaries

Location, Shape and Size of Ethiopia and the Horn

The Horn of Africa

  • The Horn of Africa → a region of eastern Africa that is a narrow tip protruding into the northern Indian Ocean, separating it from the Gulf of Aden
  • It is the easternmost extension of Africa
  • Home to the countries of:
    • Djibouti
    • Eritrea
    • Ethiopia
    • Somalia
    • [three_force_vectors.png]
  • Their cultures have been linked throughout their long history
  • Although the countries of the region share many common features, there is also great diversity among them, rendering each country unique in many respects

Location of Ethiopia

Astronomical Location (Mathematical Location)

  • Ethiopia is located between:
    • Latitude → 3°N (Moyale, south Borena) to 15°N (Badme, Tigray)
    • Longitude → 33°E (Akobo, west Gambella) to 48°E (the tip of Ogaden)

  • The East-West distance (15°) is longer than the North-South distance (12°)
Key Insight: Ethiopia's astronomical location means it lies entirely within the tropics (between the Equator and Tropic of Cancer), giving it a generally warm climate with variations due to altitude.

Implications of the Location and Size of Ethiopia

Climate

  • Ethiopia's location between 3°N and 15°N (between the Equator and Tropic of Cancer) implies that the country has a tropical climate, though modified by its altitude
  • The location of Ethiopia relative to the Indian Ocean, the Atlantic Ocean and the African and Asian landmass has also various bearings on the climate of Ethiopia
Example: The highlands of Ethiopia have a temperate climate despite being in the tropics due to high altitude, while the lowlands are hot and arid.

Socio-Cultural

  • Ethiopia is one of the earliest recipients of the major world religions:
    • Christianity
    • Islam
    • Judaism
  • This is due to Ethiopia's proximity to the Middle East
  • The linguistic and other cultural relationships which Ethiopia shares with its neighbors reflect the influence of location
Example: The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church is one of the oldest Christian churches in the world, dating back to the 4th century AD.

Political

  • The political history of Ethiopia has been considerably influenced by:
    • Geopolitical considerations of superpowers
    • Adjacency to the Red Sea → a major global trade route
    • The Middle East geopolitical paradigms
  • As a result, Ethiopia has been exposed to external invasions a number of times
  • However, the country resisted foreign intervention and remains free of external domination
Historical Example: The Battle of Adwa (1896) → Ethiopian forces defeated Italian invaders, making Ethiopia the only African country to remain uncolonized.

Size of Ethiopia

  • Ethiopia has a total area of approximately 1,106,000 square kilometers
  • It is the 8th largest country in Africa and 25th largest in the World
  • It extends about:
    • 1,639 kilometers East-West
    • 1,577 kilometers North-South
  • It contains about:
    • 0.7 percent of the world's land area
    • 3.6 percent of Africa's land mass

Advantages and Disadvantages of Ethiopia's Large Size

Advantages Disadvantages
Possess diverse agro-ecological zones Demands greater capital to construct infrastructural facilities
Variety of natural resources Requires large army to protect its territory

Indices of Compactness
  • There are various ways of measuring the shape of countries, known as the indices of compactness
  • It measures the deviation of the shape of a country from a circular shape
  • There are four most commonly used measures of compactness

Measure 1: Area-Boundary Ratio (A/B)

  • The ratio of area of a country to its boundary length
  • Rule: The higher the A/B ratio, the greater the degree of compactness → the country or territory has a long border compared to its surface area
Example: Country X with area 100,000 km² and boundary 1,000 km → A/B = 100
Country Y with area 100,000 km² and boundary 2,000 km → A/B = 50
Country X has higher compactness because it has less border relative to its area (more circular shape)

Measure 2: Boundary-Circumference Ratio (B/C)

  • It measures how far the boundary of a country approximates the circumference of a circle of its own size
Example: A perfect circle with circumference 100 km → B/C = 1.00
A country with irregular boundary → B/C will be greater than 1
The closer the ratio to 1, the more compact the shape

Measure 3: Area-Circumference Ratio (A/C)

  • It compares the area of the country with the circumference of a circle that passes touching the extreme points on the boundary of the country
  • Rule: The higher the A/C ratio, the greater the degree of compactness
Example: Two countries with same extreme points (same circumscribing circle):
Country A fills most of the circle → high A/C ratio → more compact
Country B has indented borders leaving empty spaces → low A/C ratio → less compact

Measure 4: Area-Area Ratio (A/A')

  • The area of the inscribing circle is the area of the smallest possible circle whose circumference passes through the extreme points on the boundary
Example: If a country fits perfectly inside a circle with area 100 km², and the country's area is 80 km² → A/A' = 0.80
The closer the ratio to 1, the more compact the shape

Basic Skills of Map Reading

What is a Map?

  • A map is a two-dimensional scaled representation of part or whole of the Earth's surface on a flat body such as piece of paper, blackboard, wood or cloth
  • Map reading encompasses a systematic identification of natural features and man-made features
  • Although many disciplines use maps, they have a special significance for Geographers as primary tools for displaying and analyzing spatial distributions, patterns and relations

Importance of Maps

  • Provide the basis for making geographical details of regions represented → the geographical facts of an area such as relief, drainage, settlement, etc.
  • Maps are powerful tools for making spatial analysis of geographical facts of areas represented
  • Maps are useful for giving location of geographical features by varied methods of grid reference, place naming, etc.
  • Maps are used in various disciplines like:
    • Land use planning, military science, aviation, tourism
    • Marine science, population studies, epidemiology
    • Geology, economics, history, archaeology, agriculture
  • Maps make storage of the geographical data of areas represented
  • Maps are potentially used to assess reliable measurements of geographical features → measurements can be of area size, distance, etc.

Types of Maps

  • There are many types of maps according to their purpose and functions
  • For now, only topographical and statistical maps are considered

A. Topographical Maps

  • Topographic maps depict one or more natural and cultural features of an area
  • They portray different features at the same time
  • They could be small, medium or large scale depending on the size of the area represented
  • Contents of topographical maps depend on:
    • Purpose of a map
    • Scale of a map
    • Date of compilation
    • Nature of the land represented
Example of Topographical Map: A 1:50,000 scale topographic map of Addis Ababa showing mountains (Entoto), rivers (Kebena and Akaki), roads, buildings, and vegetation all on the same map sheet.

B. Special Purpose / Statistical Maps

  • These are maps which show distribution of different aspects such as temperature, rainfall, settlement, vegetation, etc.
  • Distribution maps that emphasize the distribution of a single geographical element or character
Example of Statistical Map: A rainfall distribution map of Ethiopia showing only annual precipitation amounts across different regions (high rainfall in the southwest near Gore, low rainfall in the southeast near Ogaden).
Another Example: A population density map showing only the number of people per square kilometer across Ethiopian regions (high density in Shewa, low density in Somali region).

Marginal Information on Maps (Elements of Maps)

Title → the heading of the given map which tells what the map is all about
Key (Legend) → the list of all conventional symbols and signs shown on the map with their interpretation
Scale → the ratio between the distance on the map and the actual ground distance
North Arrow → indicated with the north direction on a map
Margin → the frame of the map; important for showing the end of the mapped area
Date of Compilation → the date of map publication
